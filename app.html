`Weather is the state of the atmosphere, describing for example the degree to which it is hot or cold, wet or dry, calm or stormy, clear or cloudy. On Earth, most weather phenomena occur in the lowest layer of the planet's atmosphere, the troposphere, just below the stratosphere. Weather refers to day-to-day temperature and precipitation activity, whereas climate is the term for the averaging of atmospheric conditions over longer periods of time. When used without qualification, "weather" is generally understood to mean the weather of Earth. Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the conditions of the atmosphere for a given location and time. People have attempted to predict the weather informally for millennia and formally since the 19th century. Weather forecasts are made by collecting quantitative data about the current state of the atmosphere at a given place and using meteorology to project how the atmosphere will change. The process of gathering and analyzing weather data to predict future weather conditions is known as weather forecasting. Weather forecasts can be short-term, covering the next few hours or days, or long-term, covering the next few weeks or months. Meteorologists use a variety of tools and techniques to make weather forecasts, including weather satellites, radar systems, computer models, and historical data. Weather affects many aspects of human life, including agriculture, transportation, construction, and recreation. Farmers rely on weather forecasts to plan their planting and harvesting schedules. Airlines use weather forecasts to determine flight routes and schedules. Construction companies monitor weather conditions to ensure worker safety and project timelines. Outdoor enthusiasts use weather forecasts to plan activities such as hiking, skiing, and boating. Weather can be described using various parameters, including temperature, humidity, atmospheric pressure, wind speed, and precipitation. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules in the air and is typically measured in degrees Celsius or Fahrenheit. Humidity is a measure of the amount of water vapor present in the air and is often expressed as a percentage. Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted by the weight of the air above a given point and is measured in units such as millibars or inches of mercury. Wind speed is the rate at which air molecules move past a fixed point and is typically measured in kilometers per hour or miles per hour. Precipitation refers to any form of water, such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail, that falls from the atmosphere to the Earth's surface. Weather phenomena can vary widely from one location to another and from one time of year to another. For example, the weather in tropical regions tends to be hot and humid year-round, whereas the weather in polar regions tends to be cold and dry. Weather patterns can also be influenced by geographic features such as mountains, oceans, and deserts. In addition to natural variability, weather patterns can also be affected by human activities such as urbanization, deforestation, and industrialization. For example, cities tend to be warmer than surrounding rural areas due to the urban heat island effect, which is caused by the absorption and retention of heat by buildings and pavement. Deforestation can alter local weather patterns by changing the surface albedo and evapotranspiration rates. Industrial emissions can affect weather patterns by releasing pollutants that interact with clouds and atmospheric circulation. Despite advances in technology, weather forecasting remains an inherently uncertain science. Meteorologists must account for numerous factors, including the chaotic nature of the atmosphere, the limitations of observational data, and the complexity of physical processes. As a result, weather forecasts often include probabilities and confidence intervals to convey the level of uncertainty associated with different outcomes.`;
